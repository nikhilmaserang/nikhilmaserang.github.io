My Thoughts On Religion


According to Wikipedia, "there is no scholarly consensus over what precisely constitutes a religion", so for my purposes I will define it as a person's views on the nature of reality - the big questions: Why am I here? What exactly is here? How did here start existing? Where does here exist? etc etc.

Personally, my worldview starts at solipsism, which basically says that I can't prove that anything outside my mind exists. In other words, "I think, therefore I am". The world outside seems so real - the grass, the wind, the people, the places - but it could all be completely fictional and fabricated. Of course, it isn't practical to live our lives claiming nothing and nobody else is real, so to function in society we have to make some assumptions, like:

  • logical reasoning doesn't lead us to false conclusions
  • our senses aren't deceiving us - what we see does truly exist where we see it
  • other people are not automatons reacting in a way indistinguishable from how a 'real' person would act; they are sentient

Of course, we should always keep in the back of our minds the possibility that nothing else is real - that those assumptions are false - but seriously entertaining it and acting as though nobody else exists isn't a wise decision.

Now, I actually used to call myself an atheist, until I realized that I had a fundamental issue with atheism: you need to have a considerable amount of faith to be an atheist. Allow me to clarify with an example:

Let's say that my religion holds that two omniscient and omnipotent Gods created the universe 13.7 billion years ago, with the exact initial conditions and rules of physics so that everything unfolds up to the present as it has, all the way up to me typing this document on my laptop. An atheist could neither prove nor disprove my beliefs, and if they asserted that my gods didn't exist they would be operating on just as much faith as me. It gets worse: what if my brother held a very similar belief but with one difference: he believed that in the beginning, there were three (rather than two) initial gods. The atheist could neither prove nor disprove my brother's beliefs either, and thus the three of us would be stuck arguing for our own positions: two gods, three gods, or no gods, with absolutely no evidence to speak of. We would all be operating on faith alone.

I'd like to point out that atheists fact-checking creationists or pointing out contradictions in scripture is perfectly fine by me - I'm not saying that theist and atheist claims have the same amount of proof or validity - but the fact that some organized religions have been very decidedly and obviously wrong about some things doesn't prove by any means that every possible religion is wrong.

Many atheists claim that they are using 100% facts and logic and there is no faith involved; many of them violently reject having even the label of 'religion' applied to atheism. However, in the words of David Eagleman, "we know too little to commit to atheism, and yet we know too much to commit to a particular religion." Both positions require faith, and as such I reject both of them as untenable. I respect those with faith, both the theist and atheist varieties (Life of Pi is one of my favorite books), and understand that it is a deeply personal decision affected by many factors - it just isn't the right path for me. However, although I respect the faithful, I do have to say that the flame wars between atheists and theists, which seem to appear to the participants to be titanic struggles between good and evil, are really quite silly, and should be avoided at all costs.

So not atheist, not theist...where does this leave me? In the example above, an agnostic - normally hailed as a middle position between theism and atheism - would assert that the answer is unknowable. A logical positivist would go even further, saying that as it is unknowable, it is meaningless to pursue that avenue of thought. However, I don't feel at home in either of those camps. Agnosticism's doctrine of not accepting anything on faith alone seems to me rather bland, while my belief that the deep questions about reality are meaningful, even if we can't assert anything about the answers, disqualifies me from being a positivist.

I was running out of options, but I fortunately stumbled onto a TED Talk by David Eagleman about his philosophy, possibilianism, which really resonated with me. My interpretation of possibilianism is as follows:

  • Human knowledge is like a pier extending into a vast ocean: we know a good deal about the world, but there is a great sea of knowledge we don't have yet.
  • The deep waters beyond the point where the horizon meets the ocean is the great unknown; these are the things that we don't even know that we don't know.
  • We know too much to commit to any particular religious doctrine, yet too little to commit to atheism; agnosticism is simply an expression of uncertainty, which is unsatisfactory.
  • We can address this issue by creating a possibility space, where each point is a possibility, an idea about the universe.
  • Hinduism, Buddhism, Sikhism, Jainism, Christianity, Islam, Judaism, and all of the other traditional faiths each have a single point in our possibility space.
  • Other worldviews, such as Andy Weir's The Egg (animated version), Last Thursdayism, or Simulation Theory each also get a single point in the possibility space.
  • I like to imagine the possibility space as a spherical shell around me, because each point on the sphere is equidistant from me at the center, which symbolizes the equal consideration given to each point.
  • Possibilianism is not "anything goes": a possibility is initially given fair consideration, but then we "import the tools of science" - data, reasoning, the scientific method, etc - to decide whether it fits the facts.
  • Possibilianism is an "active exploration of the possibility space", and is about applying the scientific method to different ideas about reality.
  • "Possibilianism reflects the scientific temperament of creativity, testing, and tolerance for multiple ideas."
  • Possibilians are comfortable holding and considering multiple conflicting possibilities in their minds without knowing which is correct.
  • "Uncertainty is an uncomfortable position. But certainty is an absurd one." - Voltaire

So I consider myself a possibilian. I think that we should use the scientific method to learn more about the world, and I think we should also consider the ideas that currently lie beyond the realm of science. Of course, we shouldn't commit to any particular idea without the data to support it, but we should be open to considering all ideas equally if we have equal supporting evidence for them. Some ideas will inevitably be superseded by others, either because of the existence of new evidence or the application of Occam's Razor (and other logical tools), and that's ok. It's all part of the possibilian mindset of being open to ideas and exploring the possibilities.
